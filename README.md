# Large Seeds, Bats, and Tents: A Neotropical Dispersal Story

ABSTRACT

The story of seed dispersal by frugivorous bats (Family: Phyllostomidae) in the Neotropics has been extensively documented over the years, with most studies focusing on small seeds that can be ingested and dispersed during flight. However, an ecologically significant group of plants bearing large-seeded fruits is also dispersed by tent-roosting bats. For this group of neotropical bats, roosting sites - commonly referred to as - “tents”, perform a crucial role in the dissemination process of large seeds. Through our systematic analysis, we highlight the understudied role of tent-roosting bats as dispersers of large seeds. Specifically, we present insights on 1) the geographical and spatiotemporal impact of studies on large-seeded dispersal by tent-roosting bats, 2) the taxonomic distribution of bat and plant species, 3) interactions between tent-roosting bats and plants with large seeds throughout a dispersal network, 4) relationships between body size and the length of the fruits, along with  a brief description of the fruits’ characteristics (shape and color), and 5) the primary uses of plants dispersed by tent-roosting bats for humans throughout the neotropical region. 

Keywords: Chiroptera, angiosperm, bipartite network, morphological traits, endangered species, ecological and provisioning service.
